Summary

*Sizwe Bansi is Dead*, co-authored by Athol Fugard, John Kani, and Winston Ntshona, is a play set in 1972 in New Brighton, a Black township in Port Elizabeth, South Africa, during the apartheid era. The play explores the dehumanising effects of apartheid laws, in particular the Pass Laws, on the lives of Black South Africans. It highlights the struggles of Black individuals to survive and maintain their identity in a system designed to oppress and control them.

The story begins in Styles’ photography studio. Styles, a Black man, engages the audience with a monologue about his life and experiences. He discusses his previous work at the Ford Factory, highlighting the exploitation and racism he faced. He then talks about the importance of his photography studio as a space for Black people to document their lives and create a sense of identity and dignity.

A customer enters the studio, introducing himself as Robert Zwelinzima. He wants to have his photo taken to send to his wife in King William’s Town. As Robert poses for the photograph, the narrative shifts, revealing his true identity as Sizwe Bansi.

Through a series of flashbacks and improvisations, Sizwe recounts his journey to Port Elizabeth in search of work to support his wife and four children. His efforts are met with rejection and discrimination due to the restrictions imposed by the Pass Laws. He is denied a work permit and given three days to leave Port Elizabeth.

Sizwe seeks refuge at his friend Buntu’s house. Sizwe expresses his despair and contemplates burning his passbook. Buntu, however, explains the futility of such an act, emphasising the pervasive reach of the apartheid system, aided by technology.

Even menial jobs like being a garden boy or selling potatoes are out of reach for Sizwe due to his lack of proper documentation. Buntu suggests that Sizwe's only option is to return to King William’s Town and work in the dangerous and poorly paid mines.

Sizwe refuses to accept this fate. He is determined to stay in Port Elizabeth and find a way to survive.

One evening, Sizwe and Buntu encounter a dead man in the street. Sizwe jokingly suggests checking the dead man's passbook for his address. Buntu, initially reluctant, retrieves the passbook and discovers the man's name is Robert Zwelinzima.

Buntu seizes this opportunity and proposes that Sizwe assume Robert’s identity by exchanging their photographs in the passbooks. Sizwe is initially hesitant, expressing his fear of losing his name and becoming "another man’s ghost”.

Sizwe's internal struggle is a central theme of the play. The Pass Laws have reduced his name to a mere label, stripping him of his individuality and humanity. He faces a profound dilemma: choose survival by sacrificing his identity or uphold his dignity while facing certain hardship and possibly death.

Buntu argues that survival is paramount, especially given Sizwe’s responsibility to his family. He emphasises that in a system that treats Black people as ghosts, assuming the identity of a dead man is a necessary act of defiance and a means to "spook them to hell”.

Sizwe ultimately chooses survival. He agrees to become Robert Zwelinzima. Buntu helps him memorise Robert’s details to avoid detection. However, Sizwe remains aware of the precarious nature of his situation, recognising that his Black skin itself is a source of trouble in apartheid South Africa.

The play ends back in Styles’ studio. Sizwe, now Robert, poses for his photograph, smiling to project an image of happiness and contentment for his wife, masking the harsh reality of his situation. The play concludes with Styles capturing this "false image" with a camera flash.

*Sizwe Bansi is Dead* is a powerful commentary on the dehumanising effects of apartheid. It explores the themes of identity, injustice, and survival through the poignant story of a man forced to choose between his name and his life. The play highlights the resilience and resourcefulness of Black South Africans in the face of oppression while simultaneously exposing the cruelty and absurdity of the apartheid system.

Characters

Characters

Sizwe Bansi

The protagonist of the play. Sizwe is a hardworking and determined man forced to leave his family in King William's Town to search for work in Port Elizabeth. Denied a work permit, Sizwe finds himself facing impossible choices in a society that sees him as less than human.

Styles

The owner of the photography studio. Styles is a witty and insightful commentator on the absurdities of apartheid. His monologues offer a glimpse into the lives of Black people under apartheid and the ways in which they found moments of joy and dignity amidst oppression.

Buntu

Sizwe's friend who offers him lodging and helps him to get a job. Buntu is a resourceful and pragmatic man who understands the harsh realities of apartheid. He devises the plan for Sizwe to assume a new identity, a plan that highlights the lengths to which Black people were forced to go just to survive.

Themes

Themes

Identity

Apartheid sought to strip Black South Africans of their identity, reducing them to nameless, faceless entities. Sizwe's struggle to hold on to his name and his sense of self in the face of this dehumanising system is at the heart of the play. His decision to assume a false identity raises questions about the nature of selfhood and the compromises people are willing to make for survival.

Injustice

The play is a searing indictment of the injustices of apartheid. The Pass Laws, designed to control the movement of Black people, are a constant reminder of the inequality and oppression that defined life under apartheid. Sizwe's experiences highlight the systemic racism and discrimination that Black South Africans faced daily.

Survival

Under apartheid, survival was a daily struggle for Black South Africans. Sizwe's desperation to find work and provide for his family is a testament to the harsh economic realities of apartheid. The play shows the extraordinary lengths to which Black people were forced to go simply to exist.
