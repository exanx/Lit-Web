Overview

"Sonnet 73" is one of 154 sonnets written by William Shakespeare. It is part of the "Fair Youth" sequence, addressed to a young man. The sonnet explores the themes of aging, mortality, and the enduring power of love.

Summary

The speaker compares himself to the late stages of autumn, the dying embers of a fire, and the twilight of the day, all symbolic of his approaching death. He acknowledges his physical decline but finds solace in the love of his addressee, who cherishes him all the more knowing that their time together is limited.

Line-by-Line Analysis

Quatrain 1

That time of year thou mayst in me behold
When yellow leaves, or none, or few, do hang
Upon those boughs which shake against the cold,
Bare ruin'd choirs, where late the sweet birds sang.

  • The speaker opens with a direct address to the addressee, drawing attention to his aging self.
  • He uses a metaphor, comparing himself to late autumn, when leaves are falling and trees are bare. The description of "yellow leaves, or none, or few" suggests a sense of depletion and decay.
  • The imagery of "boughs which shake against the cold" emphasizes the harshness of approaching winter and, symbolically, the speaker's frailty.
  • The final line introduces a more complex metaphor. The "bare ruin'd choirs" refer to the branches of the trees, now devoid of birdsong, suggesting a loss of vitality and beauty. This image could also allude to the decay of churches and religious institutions, hinting at a broader reflection on mortality.

Quatrain 2

In me thou see'st the twilight of such day
As after sunset fadeth in the west,
Which by and by black night doth take away,
Death's second self, that seals up all in rest.

  • The speaker continues the theme of decline, now comparing himself to the twilight, the period between sunset and night.
  • The imagery of fading light reinforces the sense of transience and the speaker's awareness of his dwindling time.
  • The personification of "black night" taking away the twilight creates a sense of inevitability and finality.
  • Night is described as "Death's second self," a powerful metaphor that equates sleep with death, both bringing an end to activity and awareness.

Quatrain 3

In me thou see'st the glowing of such fire
That on the ashes of his youth doth lie,
As the death-bed whereon it must expire,
Consum'd with that which it was nourish'd by.

  • The final comparison is to a dying fire, its embers glowing on the "ashes of his youth."
  • The fire, like the speaker, is nearing its end, its fuel (youth) almost spent.
  • The metaphor of the "death-bed" further emphasizes the imminence of death.
  • The final line contains a paradox: the fire is "Consum'd with that which it was nourish'd by." This could be interpreted as the speaker being consumed by the very experiences that once sustained him, suggesting that life ultimately leads to its own demise.

Literary Devices

  • Metaphor: The speaker uses three extended metaphors to compare himself to late autumn, twilight, and a dying fire, all symbolic of his approaching death.
  • Imagery: The poem is rich in visual imagery, evoking the fading light of twilight, the bare branches of trees, and the glowing embers of a fire.
  • Personification: "Black night" is personified as an entity that takes away the twilight.
  • Paradox: The fire being "Consum'd with that which it was nourish'd by" is a paradox that suggests life leading to its own demise.
  • Structure: The sonnet follows the traditional Shakespearean structure of three quatrains and a couplet, with a rhyme scheme of ABAB CDCD EFEF GG.

About the Author

William Shakespeare (1564-1616) was an English poet, playwright, and actor, widely regarded as the greatest writer in the English language and the world's greatest dramatist. He is often called England's national poet and the "Bard of Avon". His extant works, including collaborations, consist of some 39 plays, 154 sonnets, two long narrative poems, and a few other verses, some of uncertain authorship. His plays have been translated into every major living language and are performed more often than those of any other playwright.

Themes

  • Aging and Mortality: The sonnet is a meditation on the speaker's awareness of his own mortality. The metaphors of autumn, twilight, and a dying fire all point to the inevitability of death.
  • The Power of Love: Despite the speaker's preoccupation with death, the sonnet ends on a note of hope and affirmation. The addressee's love is strengthened by the knowledge of the speaker's mortality, making their time together all the more precious.
  • Time and Transience: The poem emphasizes the fleeting nature of time and the inevitability of change. The imagery of fading light and dying embers reinforces the idea that all things must pass.
