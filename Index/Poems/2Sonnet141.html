Overview

"Sonnet 141" is one of 154 sonnets written by William Shakespeare. It belongs to the "Dark Lady" sequence, a group of sonnets addressed to a woman with whom the speaker has a complex and troubled relationship. The sonnet explores the themes of love, desire, reason, and the conflict between the heart and the mind.

Summary

The speaker begins by stating that he does not love his mistress with his eyes, as they see many flaws in her. However, his heart loves her despite these flaws, defying his reason and senses. He acknowledges that she does not possess qualities that would typically attract him, but he remains inexplicably drawn to her, finding a strange pleasure in his own suffering.

Line-by-Line Analysis

Quatrain 1

In faith, I do not love thee with mine eyes,
For they in thee a thousand errors note;
But 'tis my heart that loves what they despise,
Who, in despite of view, is pleas'd to dote.

  • The speaker opens with a direct declaration: "In faith, I do not love thee with mine eyes." This sets up the central conflict of the sonnet—the disconnect between his reason and his emotions.
  • He acknowledges that his eyes "a thousand errors note" in his mistress, suggesting that she is physically imperfect or perhaps even morally flawed.
  • The turning point comes in the third line: "But 'tis my heart that loves what they despise." This highlights the paradox at the heart of his feelings: his heart loves her despite her flaws, going against the judgment of his eyes.
  • The final line emphasizes the heart's willful blindness, choosing to "dote" on her "in despite of view," meaning regardless of what his eyes see.

Quatrain 2

Nor are mine ears with thy tongue's tune delighted;
Nor tender feeling to base touches prone,
Nor taste nor smell desire to be invited
To any sensual feast with thee alone.

  • The speaker continues to list the ways in which his mistress fails to meet conventional standards of beauty or attraction. Her voice does not delight his ears, nor does her touch appeal to his sense of feeling.
  • He further emphasizes the lack of sensual attraction by stating that his senses of taste and smell have no desire to be "invited to any sensual feast" with her. This reinforces the idea that his love is not based on physical or superficial qualities.

Quatrain 3

But my five wits nor my five senses can
Dissuade one foolish heart from serving thee,
Who leaves unsway'd the likeness of a man,
Thy proud heart’s slave and vassal wretch to be.

  • The speaker acknowledges the powerlessness of both his "five wits" (intellect, imagination, fantasy, estimation, memory) and his "five senses" to dissuade his heart from loving her. This further underscores the irrationality of his love.
  • He describes his heart as "foolish" for persisting in its devotion despite the lack of any logical reason.
  • The final two lines contain a series of metaphors that depict the speaker's debasement in love. He becomes less than a man, a mere "slave and vassal wretch" to her "proud heart."

Couplet

Only my plague thus far I count my gain,
That she that makes me sin awards me pain.

  • The couplet introduces a final paradox. The speaker finds a perverse "gain" in his "plague" of love.
  • The pain she inflicts upon him, perhaps through her unfaithfulness or indifference, becomes a source of twisted pleasure. This could suggest a masochistic element to his love or a sense of self-punishment for succumbing to such an irrational passion.
  • The final line also hints at a moral dimension, as he acknowledges that his love for her is a "sin." The nature of this sin is left ambiguous, but it could refer to her unworthiness as a love object or the adulterous nature of their relationship.

Literary Devices

  • Paradox: The poem is built on a series of paradoxes, such as the speaker's heart loving what his eyes despise and his finding gain in his plague of love.
  • Metaphor: The speaker uses metaphors to describe his debasement in love, comparing himself to a slave and vassal.
  • Personification: The speaker's heart is personified as a separate entity with its own will and desires, capable of defying reason and the senses.
  • Structure: The sonnet follows the traditional Shakespearean structure of three quatrains and a couplet, with a rhyme scheme of ABAB CDCD EFEF GG.
  • Imagery: The poem employs vivid imagery, particularly in the second quatrain, to emphasize the lack of sensual attraction the speaker feels for his mistress.

About the Author

William Shakespeare (1564-1616) was an English poet, playwright, and actor, widely regarded as the greatest writer in the English language and the world's greatest dramatist. He is often called England's national poet and the "Bard of Avon". His extant works, including collaborations, consist of some 39 plays, 154 sonnets, two long narrative poems, and a few other verses, some of uncertain authorship. His plays have been translated into every major living language and are performed more often than those of any other playwright.

Themes

  • The Irrationality of Love: The sonnet explores the idea that love can be irrational and defy logic and reason. The speaker's heart loves his mistress despite her flaws and the lack of any obvious attraction.
  • The Conflict Between Heart and Mind: There is a constant battle between the speaker's heart and mind. His heart leads him to love someone whom his mind knows is unworthy.
  • Obsessive Love and Pain: The speaker's love borders on obsession, and he finds a strange pleasure in the pain she inflicts upon him. This suggests a complex psychological dynamic and perhaps a masochistic element to his love.
  • Love and Sin: The final line hints at a moral dimension to the speaker's love, suggesting that it is somehow sinful. This adds a layer of complexity and raises questions about the nature of their relationship and the social norms of the time.
