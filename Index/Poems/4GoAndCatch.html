Summary

"Go and Catch a Falling Star" is a poem by John Donne in which the speaker issues a series of impossible commands, ultimately culminating in a cynical statement about the impossibility of finding a woman who is both beautiful and faithful. The speaker's tone is one of sardonic wit, suggesting a jaded view of love and fidelity.

Line by Line Analysis

Go and catch a falling star,
This opening line sets the tone of the poem with a hyperbolic and impossible task. It introduces the motif of unattainability.
Get with child a mandrake root,
Another impossible task, referencing the mythical mandrake root, believed to have magical properties and a human-like form. This line reinforces the theme of the unattainable and introduces the idea of deception through the mandrake root's association with witchcraft.
Tell me where all past years are,
This line shifts to a more philosophical concern, pondering the passage of time and the impossibility of retrieving the past. It suggests a sense of loss and the ephemeral nature of life.
Or who cleft the devil's foot,
A seemingly nonsensical task that again emphasizes the absurd and unattainable. It could be interpreted as a questioning of the nature of evil and the difficulty of understanding its origins.
Teach me to hear mermaids singing,
This line introduces the motif of deception and allure through the mythical image of mermaids, known for their enchanting voices that lured sailors to their deaths.
Or to keep off envy's stinging,
This line speaks to the human experience of jealousy and the difficulty of escaping its painful effects. It grounds the poem in a relatable emotion while still maintaining the theme of impossibility.
And find
What wind
Serves to advance an honest mind.
These lines continue the quest for the impossible, seeking the elusive force that can promote honesty in a world rife with deceit. The enjambment creates a sense of anticipation and frustration, mirroring the difficulty of finding such a "wind."

The second stanza continues with the theme of impossibility, this time focusing on the search for a "true and fair" woman. The speaker suggests that even after an extensive search ("Ride ten thousand days and nights"), one will only return with the confirmation that such a woman does not exist.

The final stanza shifts to a more cynical tone, with the speaker stating that even if one were to find a "true and fair" woman, she would prove to be unfaithful by the time he is informed of her existence. This conclusion reinforces the speaker's jaded view of love and the pervasiveness of deceit in human relationships.

Literary Devices

  • Hyperbole: Exaggeration for emphasis, seen in the impossible tasks listed throughout the poem.
  • Allusion: References to mythical figures and concepts, such as the mandrake root and mermaids, enrich the poem's meaning and connect it to broader cultural traditions.
  • Metaphor: The "falling star" serves as a metaphor for the idealized but unattainable woman.
  • Imagery: The poem uses vivid imagery drawn from various fields, such as astronomy, sailing, and mythology, to create a sense of wonder and emphasize the poem's themes.
  • Irony: The speaker's seemingly earnest quest ultimately leads to a cynical conclusion, highlighting the gap between expectation and reality.

About the Author

John Donne (1572-1631) was an English poet and cleric known for his complex and intellectually stimulating poetry. He is considered a key figure in the Metaphysical school of poetry, which was characterized by its use of conceits, intricate metaphors, and a focus on philosophical and religious themes. Donne's poetry often explores themes of love, death, faith, and the human condition.
