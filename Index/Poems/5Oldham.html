Summary

"To the Memory of Mr. Oldham" is a poem by John Dryden, written as an elegy to commemorate the untimely death of the young poet John Oldham. Dryden expresses his grief and admiration for Oldham's satirical talent, comparing him to renowned figures from classical literature and lamenting the loss of his potential.

Line by Line Analysis

Farewell, too little, and too lately known,
This opening line sets the mournful tone of the poem, expressing the speaker's regret for the short time he knew Oldham and the realization that their acquaintance ended prematurely.
Whom I began to think and call my own:
This line highlights the growing bond between Dryden and Oldham, suggesting a sense of mentorship and shared literary kinship. The speaker's use of the possessive pronoun "my" emphasizes the personal loss he feels.
For sure our souls were near allied, and thine
Nor kept the laws of time, nor bounds of line.
These lines express a belief in a spiritual connection between the two poets, transcending physical limitations. The use of the metaphors "laws of time" and "bounds of line" suggest that their shared passion for poetry united them beyond the constraints of age and social standing.
Thou hadst a soul 'twas fitter to inspire
Than to be bound by metre, or by rhyme.
These lines praise Oldham's natural poetic talent, suggesting that his innate ability exceeded the technicalities of verse. Dryden implies that Oldham's potential was boundless and his spirit soared beyond the confines of conventional poetic forms.
Loosely, but strongly, flowed the numbers forth,
When in the first heat of thy generous worth,
They were struck out: at noble heat and rage
Didst thou in thy first youth engage;
These lines describe the raw power and passion of Oldham's early poetry. The imagery of "flowed" and "struck out" conveys a sense of spontaneity and unrestrained creativity, while the phrases "generous worth" and "noble heat" emphasize the sincerity and intensity of Oldham's writing.
Above the trifling cares, and low design,
Which those should mind who courted so much thine.
These lines contrast Oldham's lofty aspirations with the petty concerns of other writers who sought his approval. Dryden implies that Oldham's artistic integrity set him apart from those who were motivated by fame or social climbing.
Amazing strength was in thy untamed youth,
Thy generous fury, kind as raging fire.
These lines reiterate the forcefulness of Oldham's youthful talent, characterized by a "generous fury" that burned with passion and sincerity. The paradoxical comparison to "raging fire" suggests that Oldham's intensity was both powerful and benevolent.
But surely it was something more than rage
Which from thy birth did animate thy stage.
These lines acknowledge that Oldham's poetic drive stemmed from a deeper source than mere anger or rebellion. Dryden suggests that his talent was innate and destined for greatness from the beginning.
Thy generous fruits, though gathered ere their prime,
Still showed a quickness; and maturing time
But mellows what we write to the dull sweets of rhyme.
These lines lament the premature loss of Oldham's potential, comparing his works to "generous fruits" harvested before they reached full ripeness. Dryden implies that time would have refined Oldham's talent, but also acknowledges the inherent limitations of conventional poetry, referring to it as the "dull sweets of rhyme."

The poem continues with allusions to Virgil's Aeneid, comparing Oldham to Nisus and Marcellus, figures who also met untimely deaths. Dryden uses these classical references to elevate Oldham's status and universalize the theme of loss.

Literary Devices

  • Elegy: The poem follows the conventions of an elegy, a mournful poem written to commemorate the dead, expressing grief, praise, and consolation.
  • Metaphor: The poem employs metaphors to describe Oldham's talent and potential, such as "laws of time," "bounds of line," "generous fruits," and "raging fire."
  • Allusion: Dryden makes allusions to Virgil's Aeneid, referencing the characters of Nisus and Marcellus to draw parallels between their fates and Oldham's.
  • Personification: Dryden personifies Oldham's poetic inspiration as a "soul" that "did animate thy stage."
  • Paradox: The poem uses paradox to describe Oldham's passionate yet benevolent nature, referring to his "generous fury" as "kind as raging fire."

About the Author

John Dryden (1631-1700) was a prominent English poet, dramatist, and literary critic of the 17th century. He is considered a key figure in the development of English literature and is known for his satirical works, plays, and translations. Dryden was appointed Poet Laureate in 1668 and is credited with establishing the heroic couplet as a dominant form in English poetry.
